Where do we come from? by Ernst Muldashev

...

Ernst Muldashev’s Where Do We Come From? explores humanity’s origins through a path that winds far beyond conventional science. Blending expedition notes, ancient legends, Tibetan mysteries, and esoteric symbols, Muldashev searches for clues hidden in sacred places, lost civilizations, and secret teachings. The book suggests that our beginnings may lie in an ancient lineage — guarded, forgotten, or deliberately concealed. Part investigation, part spiritual journey, it challenges readers to consider a world where myth, history, and metaphysics intersect. At its core, it asks the timeless question with renewed urgency: Where did we truly come from?

Ernst Rifgatovich Muldashev (born 1948) is a Russian ophthalmologist and surgeon. He is best known for his work in reconstructive eye surgery and for developing innovative transplant techniques. Within the medical field, he gained recognition for combining clinical practice with experimental research.

Alongside his medical career, Muldashev led expeditions to Tibet, the Himalayas, and other remote regions. These journeys focused on ancient legends, sacred sites, and unexplained archaeological structures. His research interests gradually expanded beyond medicine into anthropology, mythology, and spiritual traditions.

Muldashev is the author of several bestselling books in which he presents alternative theories about human origins and lost civilizations. Blending scientific language with speculative ideas, his work has sparked both fascination and controversy. He remains a distinctive figure at the crossroads of science, mysticism, and exploration.

In "Where Do We Come From?", Ernst Muldashev challenges the conventional understanding of human evolution by suggesting that modern humanity may not be the first advanced civilization on Earth. He proposes that ancient beings with higher knowledge existed long before recorded history and that traces of their presence remain hidden in remote regions and sacred traditions. According to Muldashev, evolution may have been guided, interrupted, or preserved by forces beyond simple biological development.

A central theory in the book revolves around the existence of ancient humans or beings who entered a state of suspended animation, often referred to as the “sleeping people.” Muldashev claims these beings are preserved in hidden locations such as underground chambers in Tibet and the Himalayas, guarded by spiritual orders. He connects this idea to legends of Shambhala and Agartha, suggesting these places function as vaults of ancient knowledge and biological preservation.

Muldashev further theorizes that humanity has undergone multiple cycles of rise and collapse, triggered by global catastrophes such as floods, climate shifts, or cosmic events. He argues that ancient myths, religious symbols, and sacred architecture encode memories of these lost eras. The book ultimately presents humanity as part of a repeating cosmic pattern — watched, guided, and periodically reset — leaving the reader to question whether the past is truly gone, or merely hidden from view.
