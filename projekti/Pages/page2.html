Le Pater by Alphonse Mucha

...

Alphonse Mucha’s Le Pater (1899) is often considered the artist’s spiritual masterpiece- a deeply symbolic and mystical work that stands apart from his more commercial Art Nouveau posters. Created at a turning point in his life, Le Pater was Mucha’s personal exploration of faith, human struggle, and divine guidance. At his own request, only 510 copies were ever printed, making it one of the rarest and most sought-after pieces in his entire oeuvre. The book presents a series of intricate illustrations and luminous plates, each one inspired by and corresponding to a specific line of the “Our Father” prayer. Through this structure, Mucha transforms a familiar Christian text into a universal meditation on hope, morality, and the path toward spiritual enlightenment. A lot of symbols and characters still give off the same stories as told in the "Where do we come from" by Ernst Muldashev

"Our Father who art in Heaven." A monumental presence looms across the scene - a celestial figure whose face is half-shadow, half-light. Below, figures on earth look up with awe and uncertainty as though seeking both protection and revelation. The upward gaze of the central woman suggests yearning - a desire to glimpse the divine that remains just out of reach, as though the heavens are a tapestry of secrets meant only for those bold enough to search.

"Hallowed Be Thy Name." People climb a winding, impossible stone path toward a radiant woman-shaped light at the top. They stretch their arms toward her, reaching in awe and trembling, as though the holiness they seek i s both beautiful and frightening. The shining figure stands still, half-human and half-pure illumination, her presence casting spirals of sacred patterns into the landscape. The pilgrims look torn between devotion and fear of what the divine might reveal.

"Thy Kingdom Come, Thy Will Be Done."A single woman sits with her head tilted back, eyes closed, while a column of swirling, smoke-like energy rises from her head. The vapor twines upward into ornate patterns, as if her thoughts, prayers, or spirit are being pulled into the heavens. Her posture is peaceful yet powerful. Other people who have not yet reached the enlightement seem to be enchanted by the woman. This image feels like a moment of surrender where human will dissolves into divine intention - a transmission between earth and the unseen.

"On earth as it is in Heaven." A motherly figure stands with her arms open, protective and calm, while a small child hides at her legs, clinging to her for safety. Her open stance feels like a shelter — a human echo of divine protection. Ornamental patterns and ethereal shapes swirl around them, suggesting that the same forces shaping heaven move gently through the earthly bond between mother and child.

"Give Us This Day Our Daily Bread" A massive, maternal deity stands towering above the people, her chest adorned with multiple breasts from which streams of milk pour downward like sacred rivers. Below, men, women, and children kneel on the ground, drinking the falling milk as though receiving life directly from a divine source. Above this scene floats a luminous woman in white, almost angelic, her body glowing as she holds something sacred to her chest - a symbol of spiritual nourishment watching over the physical one. The entire image blends the primal and the holy: earthly hunger fed by a mythic mother, while a heavenly guardian hovers above, reminding the viewer that sustenance is both a physical need and a spiritual gift.

"Forgive Us Our Trespasses"A towering angelic figure with wide, powerful wings rises at the center of the scene, both arms lifted upward as if calling down judgment or mercy from above. His presence is immense — radiant, commanding, impossible to ignore. At his feet stand smaller human figures: one man looks up at the angel with a mixture of fear and pleading, as though begging for forgiveness or understanding. Clinging tightly to this man’s legs is another figure, half-hidden, curling into himself in terror — a childlike, frightened soul seeking safety from the overwhelming divine presence. The contrast is sharp: the angel’s vast, upright form radiates cosmic power, while the humans below reveal the trembling vulnerability of guilt, fear, and hope. (See last plate below)

"Lead us not into temptation, But deliver us from evil. Amen" Last Plate
A central figure stands with eyes closed, calm and vulnerable at the same time - as if surrendering themselves fully to trust. Just behind them hovers a holy, luminous being, almost like a guardian spirit. This protective presence leans gently forward, wrapping the central figure in a shield of light. Behind them both, shadowy creatures and twisted forms lurk in the darkness - the embodiments of temptation and hidden dangers. Their shapes reach forward but stop just short, unable to cross the invisible barrier created by the guardian’s light. The whole scene feels like a moment of quiet spiritual rescue a human soul standing still, eyes closed, while an unseen divine protector keeps the darkness at bay.
